The Deepwater Horizon Oil Spill¶

In the spring of 2010, the Deepwater Horizon, an oil rig in the Gulf of Mexico, exploded and sank causing one of the largest oil spills in history. The rig was situated over an oil well that was about fifteen-hundred meters below sea level. On April 20th, a swell of natural gas rushed through a recently installed concrete core that was installed to seal the well. The natural gas traveled up to the rigs platform where it killed almost a dozen workers and injured several more. On April 22nd,the rig sank which broke the machine that was responsible for balancing out the upward force of the gas and oil. Without its opposing force, oil began seeping into the gulf. The U.S. government estimated that approximately sixty-thousand barrels of oil were discharged per day. The following analysis will examine the data and total oil that affected the Gulf.

How reliable do you think your data are and why? Can any real-life data be 100% reliable? What could have led to errors in a particular data set?

I feel the data is only somewhat reliable especially because they are estimates. While a great amount did spill into the Gulf with an estimate of 60,000 barrels of oil each day, we don't know for sure if that is the true amount that spilled. Especially because it wasn't measured and recorded, that provides an extra margin of error. Even when we do record and study data as accurately as possible, no data can be 100% reliable. There will always be some unknown bias or factor that is unaccounted for. We never know if studies will be 100% as most of the time they are sample studies. Samples can never account for the whole population studied, be it people or barrels of oil spilled. Therefore, all studies are always prone to at least a small margin of error. In our data set, we don't know if the oil leaked from multiple locations under the water or there is leakage that is not accounted for. Again, there can be unknown factors in any data set. When you record data, you can never get a full record of that data either. On day 1, our data says the equivalent of 5000 barrels of oil spilled from the oil rig into the Gulf. Was it exactly 5000.0000... barrels? Most likely no. Our data above and in many studies are approximations, even when using the most advanced technology to measure something. There can always be an extra decimal place that is missed or isn't measured. 5.5 can be a lot different than 5 in a data set. Thus, errors are very common in research, no matter how small, and shows that real life data isn't always 100% reliable.

As Figure 5 shows along with the curve fitting from Figure 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3, a different approach to obtaining the best equation for the data from Table 1 is necessary. Figure 5 shows how from Day 1 to about Day 44, there is almost a constant slight decrease in the difference between consecutive points. However, from Day 45 onward, the data becomes a lot less predictable. From Day 45 to around Day 55, we can see that the difference between those ten days are significantly lower, but also closer to each other than from the rest of days. This is also exhibited from Day 56 to Day 79, and again from Day 80 to 85.

  • Overall Average Change (Day 1 to 85): 47860.12941
  • Average Change From Day 1 to Day 44: 58358.79545
  • Average Change From Day 45 to Day 55: 44472.81818
  • Average Change From Day 56 to Day 79: 31775.66667
  • Average Change From Day 80 to Day 85: 40995.4

As we can see, the overall average change could never speak for the data as a whole because the four subsections of days clearly have varying average changes, from 60k to 45k to 30k and back to 40k. Therefore, we have define a piecewise function for each of these intervals. Since cubic curve fitting seemed to fit the data the best before, we'll use the same fitting on each interval. However, since the average change from [1,44] is fairly constant, linear curve fitting on this interval will prove more than enough.

Therefore, we use a cubic curve fit on [45,85]. A quadratic curve fit doesn't fit well on [45,85]. A quintic curve fitting probably would've worked even better than a cubic, but the cubic fits well enough. Thus, we have a piecewise function that is defined as so:

$$ f(x) = \begin{cases} 59639.03x - 37726.59 & 1 \leq x\leq 44 \\ 12.40x^{3} - 2568.12x^{2} + 208080.14x - 2686701.45 & x \geq 45 \end{cases} $$

There can be various different reasons why we find the data exhibiting this sort of behavior where it was constant for about 45 days and then all of a sudden started being more varied. The main reason would be that action started to be taken in between the 44th and 45th day. On May 2, 2010 Transocean started drilling the first relief well, which is 44 days away from April 20, 2010, when oil started spilling. Because of this relief, the recorded barrels of oil started to go down. On May 16, around the 58th day, a second relief well started to be drilled which would be the cause of the third interval, from day 56 to 79, decreasing in average oil barrels spilled each day. On July 15, 2010 the well was capped which was around the 85th day since the beginning of the oil spill and thus marked the end of oil being released. However, if the oil was to continue spilling, the amount of oil leaking would have to continue following the function $ f(x) $ and predict how much more would be released. Since the piecewise function $f(x)$ in Figure 6.2 fits the graphed data the best out of all the other curve fitting (Figure 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, 6.1), Figure 6.2 would be our best model and $f(x)$ be our best-fit equation.

Using the best model, we can estimate the amount of oil on August 1, 2010. On July 15, 2010, the 85th day, there was about 4 million total barrels of oil leaked. To find the amount of oil on August 1, 2010, we have to use the piecewise function $f(x)$. If July 15 was the 85th day, that means August 1 was the 102nd day and applying that to our equation:

$$ f(102) = \begin{cases} 59639.03x - 37726.59 & 1 \leq x\leq 44 \\ 12.40x^{3} - 2568.12x^{2} + 208080.14x - 2686701.45 & x \geq 45 \end{cases} $$

$$f(102) = 12.40(102)^{3} - 2568.12(102)^{2} + 208080.14(102) - 2686701.45$$$$f(102) = 4977731.55$$

This mean that on the 102nd day, August 1, 2010, 497731.55 total barrels of oil, almost 5 million, will have been spilled and on the surface. Thus according to my model, within 17 days, 1 million more total barrels of oil will have spilled.

Derivative of Best Fit Model¶

Our current model is:

$$ f(x) = \begin{cases} 59639.03x - 37726.59 & 1 \leq x\leq 44 \\ 12.40x^{3} - 2568.12x^{2} + 208080.14x - 2686701.45 & x \geq 45 \end{cases} $$

Because it is a piecewise function, we have to find the derivate on the two different intervals so:

$$ f'(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{d}{dx}(59639.03x - 37726.59) & 1 \leq x\leq 44 \\ \frac{d}{dx}(12.40x^{3} - 2568.12x^{2} + 208080.14x - 2686701.45) & x \geq 45 \end{cases} $$

The derivative of our best fit model is:

$$ f'(x) = \begin{cases} 59639.03 & 1 \leq x\leq 44 \\ 37.2x^{2} - 5136.24x + 208080.14 & x \geq 45 \end{cases} $$
